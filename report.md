## Meeting Recap Prototype Report

I built this prototype to automate the tedious post-event task of turning sprawling executive seminars into tidy, executive-ready briefings. The business use case is straightforward: internal events often run long, feature informal cross-talk, and require dedicated admin time to translate those conversations into structured summaries for leadership. Automating the cleaning, highlighting of decisions, and action-item tracking preserves consistency, reduces latency between meeting close and report delivery, and frees the administrative team to focus on higher-level coordination.

The model I chose is OpenAI’s `gpt-5.2` via the LangChain `ChatOpenAI` wrapper. Its fluency in English reasoning, combined with the ability to control temperature and capture multi-part prompts, aligns with the need for polished, third-person prose and detailed structural output. `gpt-5.2` also handles embedded instructions (e.g., “highlight contrast”) well, making it easier to surface metrics like original and compressed lengths directly inside the response.

Baseline vs. final design:
1. The baseline prompt was minimal—“You are a professional assistant that summarizes meeting notes clearly and concisely”—so the model often drifted toward a generic tone and ignored structural goals like length contrast or explicit summaries of its own work. Key decisions could be missing, and the analogy to an “executive secretary” never surfaced.
2. Iterating the prompt (see `prompts.md`) layered on the secretary persona plus strict guardrails about [To Be Determined], filler removal, and third-person narration. The final version adds explicit instructions to record original/compressed lengths and to describe the model’s actions. Those additions consistently produced outputs that hit the business goals: more formal language, visible length metrics, and an embedded “LLM actions” statement that explains what the assistant did.

The prototype still fails when the API call cannot complete (network or authentication failures). In those cases, it falls back to a lightweight transcript-based recap, which is a safety net but lacks the full language polish and inference quality of a live LLM response. Human review is still required for ambiguous statements—especially when disagreements happen—because the model may omit nuance or fail to flag contradictory dates unless explicitly told to.

I would recommend deploying this workflow in controlled internal contexts where the transcript source is reliable and the API key is securely managed (a `.env` file excluded from version control). Before rolling it out more broadly, I would ensure the fallback summary is acceptable to users and monitor cost/quality trade-offs, but otherwise the cycle of prompt refinement plus the ability to capture contrast and self-summary makes the prototype ready for pilot use.
